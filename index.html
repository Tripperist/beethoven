The March 18 concert is the one falling within your itinerary — the exact symphony is TBC but will be published with the full season schedule.

Beethoven Link

In 1812, Beethoven travelled to Linz to stay with his brother Johann, intending to break up an unsuitable relationship. He found himself unable to focus on the family drama and instead sketched the 8th Symphony here — one of his most playful and jovial works, born from a period of conflict. The irony is pure Beethoven.

The Project

Igor Levit — one of the world's great Beethoven pianists — is performing all 32 piano sonatas across the Musikverein's 2026/27 season in multiple recitals. The sonatas span Beethoven's entire creative life, from the early Op. 2 works written in his mid-twenties to the final Op. 111 (1822), two of the most sublime pieces in all of music. Op. 111 — the final sonata

The last of the 32, in C minor, has just two movements. The second — an arietta — dissolves into trills and silence. Scholars and musicians regard it as one of the most transcendent pieces ever written. Levit performing it live, in the Goldener Saal, in the anniversary year, will be extraordinary.

The Cycle

The Konzerthaus has commissioned both the Belcea Quartet and the Quatuor Ébène — two of the world's supreme chamber ensembles — to jointly traverse all 16 of Beethoven's string quartets across six concerts in the 2026/27 season. This is one of the most ambitious chamber music projects of the anniversary year. The late quartets (Op. 127, 130, 131, 132, 135) are widely regarded as the summit of the genre — music that anticipates Schubert, Brahms, and the entire 20th century.

The Late Quartets

Op. 131 in C# minor has seven movements played without a break. Schubert asked to hear it on his deathbed. Op. 132 contains the "Heiliger Dankgesang" — the "Holy Song of Thanksgiving" — slow movement that Beethoven wrote after recovering from illness. Hearing either in Vienna in 2027 will be an experience unlike any other.

About Fidelio

Beethoven's only opera — about political imprisonment, faithfulness, and liberation — premiered at the Theater an der Wien on November 20, 1805, with Napoleonic troops occupying Vienna and the aristocratic audience largely fled. Beethoven revised it twice; the final version (1814) is one of the most powerful works in the operatic repertoire. The story: Leonore disguises herself as a prison guard ("Fidelio") to rescue her husband Florestan from unjust imprisonment. It ends with the prisoners released into light and a hymn to freedom.

In 2020, for Beethoven's 250th birthday, Fidelio was staged at the Vienna Staatsoper and at the Theater an der Wien. For the 200th anniversary of his death in 2027, a staging at the Staatsoper is essentially certain.

Theater an der Wien — Anniversary Programming

Where the 5th & 6th Symphonies premiered together in 1808 · Fidelio premiered here in 1805 · Strong anniversary programming expected · Season TBC

Season TBC mid-2026 expand ↓
Mar–May 2027 · season announcement expected mid-2026

Historical significance

On December 22, 1808, Beethoven held a concert at the Theater an der Wien that is the most extraordinary single evening in the history of music. In a freezing hall, over four hours, with an unrehearsed orchestra, he premiered: the 5th Symphony, the 6th Symphony (Pastoral), the 4th Piano Concerto, the Choral Fantasy, and the Ah! Perfido aria — all on the same programme. He also premiered sections of the Missa solemnis. The 5th and 6th Symphonies on the same night, in this hall.

Fidelio also premiered here in 1805, with Beethoven living in the theatre itself for a period. The building is still active as an opera and concert venue.

Beethoven Festival Leipzig — Gewandhaus zu Leipzig

All 9 symphonies conducted by Andris Nelsons · Soloists: Lang Lang, Igor Levit, Leonidas Kavakos, Gautier Capuçon · Chamber music cycle · World premieres by Jörg Widmann · Crossover & participatory formats

★ Major festival expand ↓
Dates May 9 – 23, 2027 Gewandhaus, Leipzig · 90 min from Vienna by air

What it is

The centrepiece of the Gewandhaus zu Leipzig's 2026/27 season — a 15-day dedicated Beethoven festival marking the 200th anniversary of his death. The Gewandhaus has a unique historical relationship with Beethoven: it was the first orchestra in the world to perform all nine of his symphonies as a complete cycle during his lifetime, and it premiered the Triple Concerto, Piano Concerto No. 5 "Emperor," and other major works.

Three festival pillars

1. Symphonies & concertos — all 9 symphonies with Andris Nelsons and the Gewandhausorchester, featuring soloists Lang Lang (piano), Igor Levit (piano), Leonidas Kavakos (violin), Gautier Capuçon (cello), Pablo Ferrandes (cello), and Augustin Hadelich (violin).

2. Chamber music — an outstanding series of string quartets, piano trios, and sonata recitals by leading ensembles and soloists.

3. Contemporary perspectives — crossover, jazz, and electronic reimaginings of Beethoven, plus community participation projects including the Stegreif Orchestra and the Ninth Symphony in a Liszt arrangement for two pianos and timpani.

World premieres

Jörg Widmann has written a new orchestral work taking Beethoven's late fragment Overture on B-A-C-H as its starting point — a chain of tradition running Bach → Beethoven → Widmann. Michael Lentz has written a new libretto for Beethoven's Egmont incidental music, narrated by Tobias Moretti.

Note on itinerary fit

The festival begins May 9 — just 8 days after your current window closes on May 1. Worth seriously considering as an extension, especially since you will have heard the Ninth in Vienna (~Apr 22) and would arrive in Leipzig for symphonies, concertos, and chamber music in festival atmosphere.

Beethovenfest Bonn — 200th Anniversary Edition

The world's oldest Beethoven festival, founded 1845 · Annual September festival in Beethoven's birthplace · 2027 is its most significant edition since its founding year · ~80 concerts across Bonn and the region

September 2027 expand ↓
Dates September 2027 (exact dates TBC) Bonn, Germany — Beethovenhalle & city venues

History

The Beethovenfest was first held in 1845 to mark the 75th anniversary of Beethoven's birth — with Franz Liszt and Louis Spohr conducting, Queen Victoria and Alexander von Humboldt in attendance. It has been held annually ever since, making it the world's oldest dedicated Beethoven festival. The 2027 edition marks the 200th anniversary of his death: the most significant milestone in the festival's 182-year history.

Character

Unlike Leipzig's more traditional approach, the Beethovenfest is explicitly forward-looking — every year's programme is built around a contemporary theme connecting Beethoven to present-day artistic questions. Alongside major orchestral concerts, there are cross-genre projects, jazz, experimental formats, talks, exhibitions, and workshops. Around 80 events across three weeks, at the Beethovenhalle, the Beethoven-Haus courtyard, the La Redoute ballroom, and outdoor venues.

Venue

Multiple venues across Bonn

2026 edition as reference

The Musikverein runs its annual spring festival in April–May each year, themed around an object from its own collections. The 2026 edition — titled "Beethovens Spazierstock" (Beethoven's Walking Stick) — ran April 17 to May 3 and was built around an actual walking stick from Beethoven's collection held by the Gesellschaft der Musikfreunde. It featured Rattle, Thielemann, Järvi, Pappano, Petrenko, and Nelsons. With the 200th anniversary of Beethoven's death in 2027, the thematic focus is almost certain to be Beethoven-centred again — but the exact theme and programme have not yet been announced.

What to expect

Two to three weeks of concentrated programming across all Musikverein halls — Golden Hall, Brahms-Saal, and the four New Halls — featuring the world's leading conductors, orchestras, and soloists. The festival typically includes evening orchestral concerts, afternoon chamber recitals, late-night contemporary music, literary events, and family programmes. Given that the Musikverein already has the Missa solemnis (March 20–26) and the Dresden cycle (April 19–22) confirmed, the spring festival would follow immediately and extend the anniversary season into May.

The project

Beethoven27 brings the Belgian period-instrument orchestra Le Concert Olympique under conductor Jan Caeyers to 27 EU cities, performing 27 key Beethoven works across 2024–2027. The programming follows Beethoven's career chronologically: early works in 2025, mature works in 2026, and in 2027 the late works — the Ninth Symphony, the Missa solemnis, the late string quartets. The goal is to gather at least 27,000 listeners across Europe and carry "a universal message of peace" beyond the EU's borders.

Bonn is the founding city — the chamber music concerts launched there and return in 2027. Other Germany and Austria stops are expected but not yet confirmed for the March–May window. Watch beethoven27.com for the full 2027 schedule.
